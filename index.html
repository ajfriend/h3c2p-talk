New algorithm

Cells to polygons

new algorithm (WIP) to convert H3 cells to spherical polygons

Why a new algorithm?

  • reported bugs coming from lat/lng (floating point) comparisons
  • couldn’t emit “global” polygons
  • could be slow for large regions

idea: try a discrete approach with new H3 Directed Edge indexes

Spherical polygons

  • loops of lat/lng points define outer ring and optional holes
  • e.g., GeoJSON:
"geometry": {
  "type": "Polygon",
  "coordinates": [[
    [150, -10],
    [-150, -10],
    [-150, 30],
    [150, 30],
    [150, -10]
  ]]
}

Spherical polygons

  • edges are directed geodesic arcs between vertices
  • i.e., shortest great-circle path on sphere

Spherical polygons

  • loops divide globe into two regions
  • need a convention for which is “inside”
  • right-hand rule (RHR): walk along directed edges, inside is to your left
  • outer loops are counter-clockwise (CCW)

Spherical polygons

  • reversing vertex order chooses other region as interior
  • completely valid polygon, but bigger than a hemisphere
  • would you call that “CCW”?
  • RHR holds

Spherical polygon holes

  • zero or more holes contained in outer loop are oriented clockwise (CW)
  • still follows RHR: inside is to the left as you walk

MultiPolygon

  • just a list of polygons
  • three in this example:
    • 2 with no holes
    • 1 with 3 holes
  • flat list: no concept of one polygon being “nested” in another

Directed Edges

H3 Directed edges

  • directed edges: defined by adjacent origin and destination cells
  • flipping order reverses the edge
  • interpretation for polygons: directed arc along boundary of cell

H3 Directed edges

  • discrete representation as uint64 e.g., 1192a1072d6bffff
  • can be hashed, compared exactly
  • no floating point issues

Cell to edges to vertices

two transforms underlie the algorithm:

  • cell \(c\) \(\to\) ordered boundary edges (edges having origin \(c\))
  • edge \(\to\) vertices

single cell (base case): gives CCW loop of lat/lng points (i.e., a valid polygon)

Symmetric edge pairs

  • symmetric pair: edge and its reverse
  • when plotting multiple cells:
    • “shrink” edges to avoid overlap

Loop surgery

Keeping edges in order

  • each cell starts as an ordered doubly-linked loop of edges (CCW)
  • loop surgery: when canceling a pair, splice the two loops
    • update doubly-linked list pointers

Two cells: before surgery

cells \(A\) and \(B\) start with separate ordered loops like

\[ \begin{aligned} A:\quad \to a^- &\to a \to a^+ \to \\ B:\quad \to b^- &\to b \to b^+ \to \end{aligned} \]

  • edges \(a\) and \(b\) are a symmetric pair
  • loop surgery: canceling should merge the loops

Two cells: after surgery

removing \(a\) and \(b\) connects their neighbors, forming a single loop:

\[ \begin{aligned} \overline{AB}:\quad \to a^- \to b^+ \to \cdots \to b^- \to a^+ \to \end{aligned} \]

  • works in all cases, in any order
  • can merge or split loops

Implementation

// surgery step
a->next->prev = b->prev;
a->prev->next = b->next;
b->next->prev = a->prev;
b->prev->next = a->next;

No natural outer loop

  • answer: any of the 4 loops could be the outer
  • choice is technically arbitrary for spherical polygons
  • but there is a “natural” choice for planar polygons

Selecting an outer loop

  • problem: a rule that picks the “natural” loop for small polygons, but also works for spherical polygons
  • solution: choose the loop that encloses (via RHR) the smallest area

consider 4 loops in this polygon

Outer loop selection

for each polygon (connected component):

  • compute area enclosed by each loop
    • spherical area in \([0, 4 \pi]\)
  • smallest area \(\to\) outer loop
  • remaining loops \(\to\) holes

first use of continuous (non-discrete) math

Gosper island optimization

Compaction and Gosper Islands

discrete directed edges approach laid groundwork for one more optimization:

  • most work is in canceling symmetric edge pairs
  • what if we can skip many edges upfront?
  • idea: compact cells and initialize with “Gosper island” edge boundaries

H3 compaction

  • a complete set of children \(\to\) replace with parent cell
  • recurse up the H3 hierarchy
  • can greatly reduce number of cells (and edges…)
  • computes relatively quickly

Example: uncompacted

  • 13 cells
    • 7 form a complete set (blue)
    • 6 an incomplete subset (green)

Example: compacted

  • compaction replaces blue cells with their parent
  • green cells unchanged (not a full set)

naive cell geometry is misleading: gaps and overlaps at the boundary!

Example: gosper island

  • parent cell isn’t representing its own geometry
    • representing geometry of children
    • plot (and interpret) it that way!
    • no gaps, no overlaps

Gosper island

  • Gosper island: outline of children cells at target resolution
    • alternatively, ordered loop of directed edges
    • generalizes single cell boundary
    • skips internal edges!

  6. Emit lat/lng MultiPolygon
